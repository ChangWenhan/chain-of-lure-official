Reviewer: 4

Comments:
This paper proposes Chain-of-Lure, a narrative-based multi-turn jailbreak attack. The topic is relevant, but the current contribution is limited.

1.Numerous recent works have already explored multi-turn jailbreak attacks using attacker LLMs, contextual disguise, role-playing, and iterative refinement. CoL appears to be a narrative reformulation of these existing ideas, without a clearly demonstrated new technical contribution.

2.The proposed Toxicity Score is essentially an LLM-as-a-judge evaluation, which is already commonly used in recent jailbreak studies. The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate why TS is necessary or superior to existing semantic evaluation protocols.

3. Several closely related studies, such as CoA and CoSafe, are missing or insufficiently discussed. This makes it difficult to fairly assess the novelty and positioning of the proposed method.

Overall, the problem is important, but the novelty and evaluation contributions are not sufficiently convincing in the current version.

Reviewer: 1

Comments:
The current contribution appears somewhat insignificant, as many of its ideas seem to echo prior LLM-based attack frameworks that rely on attacker models, iterative refinement, and LLM-as-a-judge evaluation. In its current form, the paper is not sufficiently compelling or intellectually engaging for readers, so the authors should more clearly distinguish CoL from existing work and highlight what is genuinely new.

It is still unclear to me whether “Mission Transfer” is a real methodological innovation or essentially a prompt-engineering template. Although the paper claims that CoL uses unconstrained narratives, Algorithm 1 follows a fixed structure: scenario generation, role assignment, guiding details, and mock serious questions. The authors should clarify which parts are fixed and which parts are genuinely unconstrained.

The reported perfect ASR of 1.00 across many diverse black-box models is extraordinary and needs stronger evidence. The paper should provide more details about query budget, retries, best-of-(N) sampling, randomness control, and statistical confidence. In addition, AdvBench and GPTFuzz may overestimate jailbreak success, so stronger benchmarks such as HarmBench, JailbreakBench, BeaverTails-Eval, JailbreakV, or GUIDED BENCH would make the evaluation more convincing.

Since ASR in jailbreak papers is often inflated by evaluation artifacts or keyword-based metrics, I think the authors should provide a sufficient number of generated adversarial examples, including both successful and failed cases. This would allow reviewers to directly inspect whether the reported attacks are genuinely harmful and reproducible.

The paper should provide a more principled analysis of what specifically makes CoL effective beyond prior narrative-based or multi-turn jailbreak methods. Without such evidence, it is difficult to tell whether the improvement comes from the proposed design itself, the use of a stronger attacker model, repeated refinement, or the evaluation protocol.

Mathematical equations should be punctuated properly, just like regular sentences.

Reviewer: 2

Comments:
Main concern 1: The core concept of Mission Transfer appears to be the same as multi-turn task decomposition or step-by-step jailbreak techniques (e.g., Crescendo or the ActorBreaker baseline mentioned in the paper). Stripping away the narrative or storytelling wrapper, the attack ultimately boils down to breaking a major malicious query into a sequence of seemingly benign, cumulative sub-queries. The authors fail to justify how this is a paradigm shift rather than just an incremental rebranding of existing multi-turn decomposition strategies.

Main concern 2: The authors argue that Victim LLMs are highly vulnerable to the CoL because they fail to infer deep, hidden malicious intents when wrapped in a benign narrative context. However, to evaluate the attack's success, the authors propose the Toxicity Score, which relies on a third-party Helper LLM acting as a judge to assess its alignment with the attacker’s original sensitive query. If LLMs are intrinsically flawed at identifying malicious intent concealed within narratives as the attack demonstrates, how can we trust another LLM to accurately judge the true toxicity and semantic alignment of these exact same narrative-driven outputs? The judge LLM is subject to the same cognitive blind spots.

Main concern 3: The multi-turn chain optimization mechanism appears to rely on the high generative variance of LLMs rather than a principled optimization process. Since this black-box attack lacks structured feedback for rejected prompts, the Helper LLM essentially performs random narrative mutations until a prompt stochastically bypasses the safety filter. While Table VI reports a low average number of turns for successful jailbreaks, the optimization process itself (Algorithm 2) lacks a theoretical or empirical guarantee of convergence. It is unclear whether the Helper LLM is genuinely optimizing based on the failures, or merely sampling randomly within a prompt space that is dense with vulnerabilities. To prove that this iterative refinement is indeed an optimization and not just stochastic trial-and-error, the authors must compare their multi-turn method against a Random Restart baseline (i.e., generating completely new narratives from scratch without passing the failed history) under strict query budget constraints. Without this baseline comparison, achieving a reported 1.00 ASR may merely reflect the high variance of LLM generation rather than the actual effectiveness of the proposed chain-like optimization framework.

The primary motivation relies on exposing vulnerabilities in a black-box system using another black-box system, leaving the proposed method at a purely behavioral or empirical level. The paper falls short of answering why this happens at the model architecture or alignment level, making the contribution feel somewhat incremental in the heavily saturated jailbreak literature. For example, the authors claim that CoL exploits a "systemic vulnerability distinct from token-level patterns". However, there is no proof to support how this happens internally. For the open-source models tested (e.g., Llama-3), the authors would be better to provide an analysis of the internal states (e.g., attention map). When the model enters the narrative mode, are the attention heads responsible for safety alignment suppressed or diluted by the long context?

In Algorithm 2 (Line 8: "Implicitly analyze rejection issue"), the Helper LLM is assumed to know exactly how to refine the prompt. However, in real-world black-box scenarios, a victim model usually returns a generic refusal (e.g., "I cannot assist with that"). Given that the feedback is only a generic refusal, how does the Helper LLM accurately determine whether the failure was triggered by the Scenario, the Role, or the Guidance Details? The authors need to provide a failure case analysis to justify this design.

Reviewer: 3

Comments:
The manuscript addresses an important and timely problem: evaluating the vulnerability of LLMs to narrative-based jailbreak attacks. The proposed Chain-of-Lure framework is relevant to the security scope of TIFS, and the empirical results show that such attacks can achieve high success rates across multiple victim models and datasets.

However, I have several major concerns that should be addressed before the manuscript can be considered for publication. First, the novelty of the proposed framework is not sufficiently distinguished from prior LLM-based red-teaming and multi-turn jailbreak methods. The key components, including mission transfer, narrative lure generation, and chain optimization, are mostly implemented through prompt-based orchestration, and the paper does not clearly demonstrate which parts are technically new or indispensable.

Second, the experimental comparison is not fully fair. CoL uses attacker models, helper models, and multi-turn refinement, but the paper does not normalize or report query budgets, token costs, number of optimization rounds, or computational overhead across baselines. This makes the direct comparison with methods such as TAP, DRA, Darkcite, GCG, and AutoDAN difficult to interpret.

Third, the evaluation metrics need stronger validation. ASR based on refusal keywords is fragile, and the proposed Toxicity Score relies on LLM-based judgment without sufficient human annotation, inter-rater agreement, multi-judge validation, or comparison with independent safety classifiers. As a result, the reliability of the reported harmfulness evaluation remains uncertain.

Fourth, the ablation study is insufficient. The paper should separately quantify the contribution of scenario construction, role assignment, guiding details, mock questions, question reordering, and helper-based refinement. Without these ablations, it is unclear whether the performance gain comes from the proposed narrative-chain design or simply from stronger LLM rewriting and iterative prompting.

Finally, the defense analysis is preliminary. The proposed mitigation strategies should be compared with existing guardrails or safety classifiers, and the authors should report false positive rates on benign narrative prompts, as well as robustness against adaptive attackers.

Overall, the paper studies an important security problem and provides useful empirical evidence, but the current version requires major revisions to strengthen novelty claims, experimental fairness, metric validation, component ablation, and defense evaluation.
